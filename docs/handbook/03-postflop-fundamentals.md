# 03. Postflop Fundamentals

## Learning goal

Help learners understand how postflop decisions depend on ranges, board texture, and street-by-street planning. The purpose of this handbook section is not to teach every postflop branch immediately. It is to give the platform a practical teaching framework for c-betting, checking, calling, raising, barreling, value betting, and bluffing.

## Key concepts

### Range advantage

Range advantage asks which player arrives at a board with more strong hands, more overpairs, or more comfortable continuing hands on average. It is not about one exact combo. It is about whose total range connects more naturally with the board.

### Nut advantage

Nut advantage is more specific. It asks which player can show up with more of the very strongest hands. A player can have enough top-end hands to pressure the other range even when both players connect with the board somewhat often.

### Board texture

Board texture strongly affects strategy.

Useful board characteristics for training:

- dry versus coordinated
- paired versus unpaired
- high-card-heavy versus low connected
- rainbow versus two-tone versus monotone
- static versus dynamic runout potential

The training system should teach that not all flops are treated equally.

### C-betting should have a purpose

A continuation bet is not for "seeing where you are at." It should usually serve one or more clear goals:

- get value from worse hands
- deny equity from hands that can improve
- pressure a capped or weak range
- set up future streets

### Pot control and checking

Checking is not surrender by default. It can protect medium-strength hands, manage pot size, and keep a range balanced in stronger strategy models. In beginner training, checking can also simply mean that a hand is not strong enough or does not benefit enough from immediate betting.

### Value versus bluff

Every bet should lean toward one of these purposes.

- value bet: expect to get called by worse often enough
- bluff: expect better hands to fold often enough or enough equity denial to justify the play

Bad training habits usually come from unclear motivation.

### Turn and river thinking

Later streets require more planning than a single flop action.

Questions the learner should eventually ask:

- Which worse hands can still call?
- Which draws improved or missed?
- Which parts of each range became stronger or weaker?
- Is this a good barrel card?
- Is this a value bet, bluff, bluff-catch, or check-back spot?

## Practical interpretation

A practical postflop checklist for the product is:

1. Identify the preflop ranges involved.
2. Read the board texture.
3. Ask who has range advantage and who has nut advantage.
4. Decide whether betting or checking has a clearer purpose.
5. Plan how the hand may continue on later streets.

This checklist can be simplified in beginner modules but should remain visible in the explanation style.

## Common mistakes

- c-betting automatically on every board
- betting medium-strength hands with no clear purpose
- overvaluing one pair on coordinated boards
- bluffing into players or ranges that do not fold enough
- failing to notice when turn and river cards shift range advantage
- thinking only about hero's exact hand and not both ranges

## Examples of spots

### Example 1: High dry flop after raising preflop

Hero raises preflop and sees a high disconnected flop against the big blind. This is often a natural c-bet candidate because hero may retain range advantage and can deny equity from many hands.

### Example 2: Low coordinated flop in a single-raised pot

Hero c-bets automatically on a board that strongly interacts with the caller's range. The system should teach that this board deserves more caution and more checking than a dry ace-high flop.

### Example 3: River value bet decision

Hero reaches the river with a hand that beats many bluff-catchers but loses to a narrow stronger range. The question is not "Do I like my hand?" It is "What worse hands will call?"

## Simplified postflop learning path

For the early product, postflop learning should be layered:

1. Learn board texture categories.
2. Learn the difference between value, bluff, and pot control.
3. Learn simple c-bet and check heuristics.
4. Learn turn-barrel and river-value logic.
5. Add exploit adjustments later.

This helps keep the MVP educational without pretending to solve all postflop poker immediately.

## How this concept turns into training exercises

Useful training patterns:

- board texture identification quizzes
- c-bet versus check scenario drills
- value-versus-bluff classification prompts
- turn barrel questions with changing board runouts
- river bet or check-back decisions with clear range framing
- mistake tags such as `autocbet`, `overvalued-one-pair`, and `bluffed-bad-target`
